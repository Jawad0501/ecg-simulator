ECG Educational Simulator

v1.0 · Open Source

Interactive 12-Lead ECG for Medical Education

A browser-based teaching tool that generates real-time 12-lead ECG waveforms. Select from 35+ pathological conditions, adjust electrolyte and metabolite levels, and observe the resulting changes on both the 12-lead grid and the scrolling rhythm strip.

Open Simulator →

Core Features

12-Lead Display

Standard 12-Lead Grid + Rhythm Strip

All 12 leads arranged in the conventional 4×3 layout (I, II, III, aVR, aVL, aVF, V1–V6), plus a scrolling Lead II rhythm strip beneath. Tap any lead to view an enlarged, high-resolution trace with annotated intervals.

Real-Time Morphology

Pathological Waveform Generation

Each condition alters P-wave morphology, QRS width/axis/voltage, ST-segment shape and deviation, T-wave amplitude/symmetry, QT interval, and more. Transitions animate smoothly between states.

Lab & Metabolite Controls

ECG-Relevant Labs

Adjust potassium (K⁺), calcium (Ca²⁺), magnesium (Mg²⁺), glucose, creatinine, and blood pH using stepper controls. The simulator layers these metabolic effects onto the selected condition's ECG, teaching how electrolyte disturbances modify the tracing.

35+ Conditions

Pathological Condition Library

Browse cardiac (STEMI, AV blocks, bundle branch blocks, arrhythmias), metabolic/electrolyte (hyperkalemia, hypocalcemia, etc.), drug effect (digoxin, TCA, cocaine), and congenital/structural (WPW, Brugada, HCM, long QT) conditions.

Education Panels

Built-In Theory & Treatment

Every condition includes an overview, ECG findings, pathophysiology, diagnostic criteria, and a structured treatment plan with urgency classification (emergent/urgent/routine/supportive) and numbered management steps.

Enlarged Lead View

Modal Magnification

Tap any lead to open a magnified view with a high-resolution grid, calibration pulse, and real-time measurement annotations (PR, QRS, QT, R-R, heart rate). The rhythm strip view scrolls continuously in the modal.

How to Use

1

Select a Condition

Open the sidebar (left panel) and browse conditions grouped by category. Click any condition to instantly update the 12-lead ECG, theory panels, and treatment recommendations. The transition animates smoothly over 300 ms.

  • Cardiac Conditions — Normal sinus rhythm, tachycardia/bradycardia, AV blocks (1st, 2nd Mobitz I/II, 3rd), BBB (LBBB, RBBB, LAFB), atrial fibrillation/flutter, VT, LVH/RVH, STEMI (anterior/inferior/lateral/posterior), NSTEMI, old MI, pericarditis
  • Metabolic / Electrolyte — Hyperkalemia, hypokalemia, hypercalcemia, hypocalcemia, hypomagnesemia, hypothermia
  • Drug Effects — Digoxin toxicity, quinidine, TCA overdose, beta-blocker overdose, cocaine toxicity, amiodarone
  • Congenital / Structural — WPW, Brugada (types 1–3), long QT, short QT, HCM, ARVD, dextrocardia
2

Adjust Labs & Metabolites

Under Labs & Metabolites, use the + and − steppers to adjust electrolyte and metabolite values. The ECG updates in real time to reflect the combined effect of the selected condition and the lab values. A Reset All button restores the condition's default labs.

  • Potassium (K⁺) — Peaked T → PR prolongation → wide QRS → sine wave / P-wave loss
  • Calcium (Ca²⁺) — Shortens (high) or prolongs (low) the QT interval
  • Magnesium (Mg²⁺) — Low Mg prolongs QT, adds U waves, flattens T
  • Glucose — High glucose increases heart rate
  • Creatinine — Elevated creatinine prolongs PR, reduces P amplitude
  • Blood pH — Acidosis broadens QRS and augments T; alkalosis prolongs QT
3

Read the Theory Panels

Open the Theory accordion section in the sidebar. Five collapsible panels provide:

  • Overview — Concise clinical description of the selected condition
  • ECG Findings — Bullet-point list of diagnostic ECG features
  • Pathophysiology — The mechanism behind the ECG changes
  • Diagnostic Criteria & Pearls — Formal criteria and clinical tips
  • Treatment Modality — Urgency-labelled management steps with clinical notes
4

Interact with the ECG Display

The main area shows a 4×3 grid of all 12 leads on a calibrated ECG paper background (25 mm/s, 10 mm/mV). Below is a scrolling Lead II rhythm strip. Use the toggle button (▲) in the top-left corner to show/hide the sidebar on desktop, or tap the hamburger icon on mobile.

  • Tap any lead to open a magnified overlay with high-resolution rendering, a calibration pulse, and live measurement annotations (PR, QRS, QT, R-R, rate)
  • Rhythm strip scrolls continuously; tap it to enlarge in the modal
  • Print the 12-lead ECG using the Print button (hides sidebar and overlay for clean output)
  • Press Escape to close the magnified view or mobile sidebar
5

Layer Multiple Effects

When you select a condition, the lab values automatically update to match that condition's clinical profile (e.g., hyperkalemia sets K⁺ to 6.5). You can then manually adjust individual labs to simulate a patient with multiple derangements — for example, a STEMI patient with concurrent hypokalemia. The simulator layers metabolic changes on top of the condition's baseline morphology.

Tips & Notes

What does the asterisk (*) mean next to a condition name?

It means the lab values have been manually modified from the condition's default profile. The Reset All button restores the condition's expected lab values.

How do I interpret atrial fibrillation or atrial flutter?

AF shows fibrillatory baseline with irregularly irregular R-R intervals. Flutter shows sawtooth waves with an AV block ratio (adjustable via the dropdown that appears when flutter is selected). The rhythm strip is particularly helpful for seeing these patterns.

What is "beat spreading"?

When a very fast heart rate would cause beats to overlap (e.g., VT at 180 bpm), the simulator automatically spreads beats horizontally in the magnified view for clarity. You'll see a note: "Beats spread for clarity."

How do I simulate a patient with multiple conditions?

Select a base condition (e.g., anterior STEMI), then manually adjust lab values to add electrolyte disturbances. The ECG will reflect the combined pathophysiology. Note that the lab effects layer onto the condition's parameters, not the other way around.

Is this validated for clinical use?

No — this is an educational tool for teaching ECG morphology and pathophysiology. Waveforms are mathematically generated approximations. It should not be used for clinical diagnosis.
